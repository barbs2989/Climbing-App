# Washington Class 2-3 Mountaineering Routes: Hazard Documentation

## Overview

This document provides comprehensive hazard information for 20+ Washington State Class 2-3 mountaineering and scrambling routes, organized by region. Data includes peak locations, route difficulty, approach hazards, avalanche terrain, descent hazards, and season-specific concerns.

**Target Regions:**
- Wenatchee Mountains
- East Cascades
- Chelan-Sawtooth Range
- North Cascades Foothills
- Enchantments Area
- Pasayten Wilderness
- Alpine Lakes Wilderness
- Stuart Range

## Routes Included

### Wenatchee Mountains

#### 1. **Bonanza Peak - North Ridge** (Class 2)
- **Elevation:** 9,511 ft
- **Gain:** 3,800 ft | **Distance:** 13.2 km
- **Approach Hazards:** Talus/scree field on descent; loose rock creates rockfall hazard
- **Season:** July-October
- **Key Hazards:**
  - Talus stability on descent—disturbed talus triggers rockfall for parties below
  - Ridge exposure—30+ foot exposure on narrow sections; helmets recommended
  - Afternoon electrical storms—common July-August; summit by 1pm
  - Stream crossing—high water June-July

#### 2. **Bonanza Peak - West Ridge** (Class 2-3)
- **Elevation:** 9,511 ft
- **Gain:** 4,200 ft | **Distance:** 14.8 km | **Max Angle:** 38°
- **Approach Hazards:** Loose red volcanic rock; steeper than North Ridge; descent route-finding critical
- **Season:** July-October (August-September best for rock stability)
- **Key Hazards:**
  - Loose volcanic rock—unstable and slick when wet; test holds carefully
  - Increased exposure—30-50 ft drops on steeper pitches; falling risk significant
  - Route-finding descent—multiple gullies on west side; GPS/compass essential
  - Weather window—afternoon thunderstorms more likely on exposed ridge

```json
  "aspect": "N|NE|E|SE|S|SW|W|NW",
  "commitment": "I|II|III",
  "overview": "General route description",
  "beta": "Technical beta and routefinding",
  "turnaround": "Bailout conditions and turnaround points",
  "hazards": ["hazard1", "hazard2", "..."],
  "watch_out": [
    "Specific hazard call-out #1",
    "Specific hazard call-out #2",
    "..."
  ],
  "best_season": "Prose season guidance"

```

### watch_out Field (String Array)

The `watch_out` field contains detailed hazard call-outs specific to each route. Each entry is a string describing a concrete hazard:

**Format:** `"[Hazard Type] — [specific risk/condition/mitigation]"`

**Examples:**
- `"Talus stability — disturbed talus creates rockfall hazard for parties below, move quickly through loose sections"`
- `"Ridge exposure — 30+ foot exposure on narrow sections; helmets recommended"`
- `"Stream crossing — high water in June-July; consider trekking poles"`

This field is distinct from the generic `hazards` field (which uses standardized tags) and provides route-specific, actionable hazard warnings.

## Hazard Categories

### Approach Hazards
- Stream crossings (high water June-July, slick rocks)
- Talus/scree fields (loose rock, rockfall potential)
- Trail condition (erosion, washout, routing confusion)
- Weather exposure (afternoon thunderstorms)

### Technical Hazards
- Ridge exposure (falling risk, 30-100+ ft drops)
- Loose rock (volcanic, talus, Class 3 sections)
- Class 2-3 sections (hand-use required, testing holds essential)
- Wet rock (granite, lichen, moss)

### Descent Hazards
- Scree descent (acceleration, route-finding confusion)
- Loose talus (rockfall for parties below)
- Exposure (steep descent angles)
- Route-finding (multiple gullies, navigation errors)

### Seasonal Hazards
- **Early Season (June-July):**
  - Snow on higher elevations
  - High water in streams (snowmelt)
  - Avalanche terrain instability
  - Wet rock (freeze-thaw cycles)

- **Mid Season (August-September):**
  - Afternoon thunderstorms
  - Loose talus (dried, unstable)
  - Clear weather windows
  - Optimal rock conditions

- **Late Season (October):**
  - Weather deterioration
  - Early snowfall at elevation
  - Shortening daylight
  - Trail deterioration

### Altitude Hazards
- Altitude sickness (above 8,500 ft)
- High altitude exposure (Glacier Peak 10,500+ ft)
- Thin air effects on performance
- Rapid weather deterioration at height
