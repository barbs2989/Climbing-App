# Enchantment Lakes Trailhead & Base Camp Logistics Research

## 1. Trailhead Details

### Snow Lakes Trailhead (Primary Access)
**Location & Coordinates**
- Latitude/Longitude: 47.5441°N, -120.7097°W
- Region: Central Cascades, Leavenworth Area, Okanogan-Wenatchee National Forest
- Alpine Lakes Wilderness, Snoqualmie National Forest

**Directions from Leavenworth**
- From US-2 junction, head south on Icicle Creek Road (Forest Road 76)
- Drive 4.2 miles to the Snow Lakes Trailhead parking lot (on left/south side of road)
- Total distance from Leavenworth: approximately 4.2 miles

**Parking Information**
- Large paved parking lot at trailhead
- Peak season (weekends May-October): Arrive by 5:00 AM to secure parking
- Parking can be competitive during peak times
- Fee required: Northwest Forest Pass ($5 day pass or $30 annual pass) or National Park Pass
- Facilities: Outhouse available at trailhead

### Alternative Trailheads

**Stuart Lake Trailhead**
- Elevation: 3,400 feet
- Coordinates: 47.5278°N, -120.8205°W
- Access: 8.5 miles on Icicle Road, then Forest Roads 7600 and 7601 for ~4 miles
- Parking: Severely limited; may need to park miles away during peak season
- Facilities: Vault toilets only, no potable water
- Provides access to Colchuck Lake Trail #1599A

**Eightmile Lake Trailhead**
- Access via Forest Road 7601
- Parking: Limited, similar constraints to Stuart Lake
- Road conditions: Steep, becomes washboarded as season progresses

## 2. Trail Profile: Snow Lake Basin

### Snow Lakes Trail (#1553)

**Distance & Elevation**
- Distance to Snow Lakes: 14.0 miles round trip (7.0 miles one-way)
- Elevation gain: 4,300 feet
- Starting elevation: 1,300 feet
- Highest point: 5,500 feet
- Elevation gain to Core Enchantments: 4,500+ feet

**Difficulty Rating**
- Official Rating: Hard
- User Rating: 3.92 out of 5 (based on 36 WTA votes)
- Calculated Difficulty: Hard

**Trail Description**
- Crosses Icicle Creek via log bridge immediately at trailhead
- Switchbacks up the canyon with 24 switchbacks
- Trail ascends through forested terrain with alpine lake destination
- Notable feature: Dramatic downhill return (5.3 miles of constant descent)
- The downhill section produces "constant jarring and pounding" on joints

**Trail Conditions**
- Generally well-established but limited signage
- Download GPS route beforehand for navigation assistance
- Dogs prohibited on all trails in Enchantment Permit Area
- No campfires allowed

### In Snow Lake Basin & Colchuck Zone
- Multiple alpine lakes provide water sources:
  - Snow Lakes (destination)
  - Colchuck Lake
  - Tranquil Lake
  - Isolation Lake
  - Inspiration Lake
  - Perfection Lake
  - Sprite Lake
  - Leprechaun Lake
  - Viviane Lake
  - Crystal Lake

**Critical Note on Water Sources**
- Water sources are **abundant in the Colchuck and Core Zones**
- The descent from Snow Lakes to trailhead can be **pretty dry** — fill up before descent
- All water must be treated: boiling, purification tablets, or filtering required

## 4. Campsites

### Permit Zone Organization
The Enchantments Permit Area is divided into five zones with designated camping areas:

**1. Colchuck Zone**
- Located at Colchuck Lake
- Established campsites available around the lake
- Can be competitive on busy weekends to find good spots
- Allows day-hiking into the Core Zone

**2. Core Zone** (most popular)
- Camp anywhere in the upper basin (on previously impacted sites only)
- Popular established sites around:
  - Perfection Lake
  - Inspiration Lake
  - Near the Crystal/Perfection saddle
- Most scenic but limited availability

**3. Snow Zone**
- Base camp area near Snow Lakes
- Less crowded than Core but drier in descent area

**4. Stuart Lake Zone**
- Alternative access zone via Stuart Lake Trailhead

**5. Eightmile/Caroline Zone**
- Alternative northern access zone

### Camping Regulations
- **Camp only at previously impacted campsites** — never on vegetation
- All group members must camp together in one site
- Maximum group size: Check current Forest Service regulations (typically 12 people)
- Fires prohibited above 5,000 feet or within half mile of lakes
- No standing trees may be cut
- **Dispersed camping prohibited** between FR 7601 and Icicle Creek

### Nearby Campgrounds (Vehicle-Based)
- **Eightmile Campground** (first-come, first-served)
- **Lake Wenatchee State Park**
- **Nason Creek Campground**
- **Camp Howard VRBO rental** (near Leavenworth)

## 5. Weather Patterns & Seasonal Conditions

### Temperature Ranges

**May (Shoulder Season)**
- Average High: ~50°F
- Average Low: ~35°F
- Expect winter-like conditions and snow

**June**
- Average High: ~58°F
- Average Low: ~40°F
- Shoulder season; snow melting

**July (Peak Season)**
- Average High: ~62-63°F
- Average Low: ~42°F
- Driest month (< 1 inch precipitation)
- Extended daylight (~16 hours)

**August (Peak Season)**
- Average High: ~62°F
- Average Low: ~42°F
- Driest month (< 1 inch precipitation)
- 15+ hours daylight

**September**
- Average High: ~57°F
- Average Low: ~38°F
- Fewer crowds
- Golden larch season in early autumn

**October (Shoulder Season)**
- Average High: ~48°F
- Average Low: ~32°F
- Average precipitation: ~6 inches (146 mm)
- Rain or drizzle: 15 days
- Snowfall: 7 days
- First frost typically mid-October
- Reduced daylight

### Precipitation Patterns
- **Winter (Nov-Feb):** 7-12 inches per month (wettest season)
- **Spring (Mar-May):** 4-6 inches per month, with snow at higher elevations
- **Summer (Jun-Aug):** Less than 1 inch per month; July/August driest
  - Note: Light rain, hail possible even in summer
- **Fall (Sep-Oct):** Increasing precipitation, 4-7 inches per month

### Snow Conditions
- **May:** Snow patches common; lower lakes (< 6,000') thaw by late May
- **June-July:** Core Zone lakes thaw by July; Aasgard Pass typically snow-free by late June (though often later)
- **Mid-August:** Patches of snow still present on trails in Core Zone
- **October:** Snow begins accumulating; "light dusting" common by month's end
- **Early November:** 1+ foot of snow covers basin
- **Year-round:** Aasgard Pass typically has snow coverage until late June; patches year-round

### Aasgard Pass Snow Conditions
- Often snow-covered most of the year
- Typically melts out by late June (highly variable by year)
- Many years: snow remains well into July or later
- Early season attempts require crampons, ice axes, and mountaineering expertise
- Crevasses and avalanche terrain present; hidden streams create unstable snow

### Daylight Hours
- May 1: 14.5 hours
- Peak (early July): Nearly 16 hours
- November 1: Under 10 hours

### Key Hazard Alerts
- **May-June:** Winter-like conditions, snow, avalanche danger
- **July-August:** Peak season but afternoon thunderstorms possible
- **Mosquitoes:** July onwards brings "large and particularly voracious" mosquito populations; consider head nets and repellent
- **October:** Sudden temperature drops, early snow possible

## 6. Access Seasons & Trail Passability

### Primary Permit Season
**May 15 – October 31**
- Overnight camping permits required
- Lower-elevation trailheads (Snow Lakes) accessible earlier and later
- Core Zone typically inaccessible before June due to snow

### Seasonal Access Windows

**April-May (Before Official Season)**
- Road access sometimes available before May 15
- No overnight permits required (permits only required May 15–Oct 31)
- High avalanche and snow hazard
- Aasgard Pass snow-covered; mountaineering equipment required
- Exploitation: Some climbers access via Snow Lakes before May 15 to avoid lottery

**June (Early Season)**
- Lower lakes thaw by end of May
- Aasgard Pass often still snow-covered (typically melts by late June)
- Crowds moderate but building
- Some snow patches still present on trails

**July-August (Peak Season)**
- Most accessible conditions
- Aasgard Pass snow-free (usually)
- Driest months
- Peak crowds and competition for campsites
- Extended daylight hours (15-16 hours)

**September (Shoulder Season)**
- Fewer crowds than July-August
- Cooler temperatures (~57°F highs)
- Still fully accessible
- Excellent weather and visibility

**October (Late Season)**
- Shortening days (< 12 hours by month's end)
- First snow possible mid-October
- Temperatures dropping (48°F average high)
- Increasing precipitation
- Reduced crowds
- Pit toilets sometimes winterized by late October

**November-April (Winter Closure)**
- Forest Road 7601 closed to vehicles
- Non-motorized use allowed (hiking, snowshoeing, cross-country skiing)
- Roads reopen by May 15 for permit season
- Winter access minimal due to snow and avalanche danger

### Forest Road Closure Details
- **Forest Road 7601** (access to Stuart/Colchuck/Eightmile trailheads): Typically closed **November through May**
- **Snow Lakes Trailhead Road (FR 76):** Maintained longer, but check conditions
- Non-motorized use permitted during vehicle closure
- Road reopens each year by May 15 for permit season start

## 7. Permit Requirements

### Overnight Permits (May 15 – October 31)

**Permit Cost**
- $6 non-refundable application fee per lottery entry
- $5 per person per day for actual camping
- Maximum 14-day stay

**Permit Types & Allocation**

**Advanced Lottery (75% of permits)**
- Annual application window: February 15 through March 1
- Results announced: Approximately March 15
- Success rate: Less than 10% of applicants successful
- Confirmation deadline: April 1
- Print permits: Up to 14 days before trip
- Largest share of available permits

**Daily Lottery**
- Small number of permits available during season
- Applicants must be within one-mile radius of Wenatchee River Ranger District in Leavenworth, WA
- Applied for online via Recreation.gov during permit season

**First-Come, First-Served**
- Unclaimed or cancelled permits released through Recreation.gov
- Very limited availability
- Highly competitive

### Key Permit Restrictions

**One Permit Per Person Per Season**
- "Visitors may only obtain ONE permit, per season"
- Cannot have multiple overlapping permits

**Permit Zone Assignment**
Five zones available:
1. **Core Zone** — highest demand; most scenic; allows camping anywhere in upper basin
2. **Colchuck Zone** — gateway to Core; established sites around Colchuck Lake
3. **Snow Zone** — less crowded; near Snow Lakes Trailhead approach
4. **Stuart Zone** — alternative access
5. **Eightmile/Caroline Zone** — northern access

**Group Requirements**
- All group members must camp together in one site
- Check current max group size (typically 12)

### Day-Use Permits
- Free, self-issued permits available at all three trailheads
- No reservation needed
- Simply fill out permit at trailhead

### Permit Applications & Reservation Portal
- Primary source: Recreation.gov
- Advanced Lottery: [Enchantment Permit Area Advanced Lottery](https://www.recreation.gov/permits/233273)
- Daily Lottery: [Enchantment Permit Area Daily Lottery](https://www.recreation.gov/permits/445863)

## 8. Vehicle Access & Road Conditions

### Snow Lakes Trailhead Access
- **Road:** Icicle Creek Road / Forest Road 76
- **Condition:** Maintained, generally accessible
- **Parking:** Paved lot with capacity
- **Season:** Typically accessible May-October, but check conditions

### Stuart/Colchuck/Eightmile Access
- **Road:** Forest Road 7601 (Icicle Road → FR 7600 → FR 7601)
- **Length:** Approximately 4 miles of dirt road past the junction
- **Elevation:** Climbs to ~5,000 feet
- **Road Quality:** Steep; becomes washboarded as season progresses
- **Vehicle Requirements:** High-clearance vehicles recommended; 4x4 advisable if available

### Seasonal Road Closure Details
- **Forest Road 7601:** Closed November through May (typically)
- **Closure Date:** Varies by snow conditions; closes before November
- **Reopens:** May 15 each year (for permit season start)
- **Contact:** Okanogan-Wenatchee National Forest for exact status

### Winter Access (Non-Motorized)
- Hiking permitted on closed roads
- Cross-country skiing allowed
- Snowshoeing allowed
- Avalanche danger significant; mountaineering training required

## 10. Climbing Objectives & Technical Routes

### Major Peak Objectives

#### 1. Aasgard Pass (7,841')
**Route Type:** Alpine scramble/mountaineering pass

**Distance & Elevation**
- Round trip from Colchuck Lake: 12.2 miles
- Elevation gain: 4,400 feet
- Elevation of pass: 7,841 feet
- Final push: Nearly 2,000 feet gain in ~0.75 mile

**Difficulty**
- Rating: Hard (Class 3 scramble)
- Route-finding experience, agility, and excellent balance essential
- Described as "thigh-burning, chest-bursting endeavor"
- Often has false summit; route-finding required

**Key Hazards**
- Scree terrain "near vertical at times"
- Snow coverage through early July (year-round patches)
- Hidden crevasses and avalanche terrain
- Streams melting snow from beneath creating unstable conditions
- Loose rock and exposed scrambling sections
- **Fatalities:** Injuries, search and rescue missions, and numerous fatalities have occurred

**Seasonal Considerations**
- Early season (before late June): Requires crampons, ice axes, mountaineering equipment
- Snow typically clears by late June (highly variable; often much later)
- Early departure recommended to avoid midday heat when attempting full traverse
- Suitable for summer mountaineering with Class 3 rock scrambling

**Access**
- From Colchuck Lake, rock-hop around lake's head, then transition to staircase-like grade trail
- Gateway to Core Zone; essential for accessing Dragontail Peak

#### 2. Dragontail Peak (8,219')
**Route Type:** Alpine scramble with technical variations

**Primary Route: Via Aasgard Pass**
- Ascend Aasgard Pass first, then easy hike/scramble to summit from south
- Descent: Downclimb exposed, steep snow pitch at "Pandora's Box" to gain summit ridge

**Alternative Route: Via Colchuck Glacier Couloir**
- Ascend Colchuck Glacier, climb couloir from Colchuck-Dragontail col
- More technical; includes exposed downclimbing at Pandora's Box

**Base Camp Strategy**
- Colchuck Lake serves as ideal base camp for Dragontail climbing
- Provides access to alpine rock routes

#### 3. Colchuck Peak (8,038')
**Route Type:** Alpine scramble

**Route Description**
- Southeast ridge leads to gently inclined south slope
- Short hike and scramble to summit
- Accessible via Colchuck Col or Aasgard Pass approach

**Difficulty:** Class 2-3 scramble

#### 4. McClellan Peak (8,363') — Bulger Peak
**Route Type:** Alpine scramble

**Difficulty:** Up to Class 3 scramble

**Route Description**
- Approach: Via Colchuck Lake, traverse around it to Aasgard Pass, work toward Perfection Lake
- Final ascent: Short Class 3 scramble to ridge
- Summit traverse: Small ridge walk and low-class scrambling
- Summit: Notably small ("fits only two people kind of tiny")
- Psychological exposure on traverse but minimal actual fall risk

**Single-Day Objective:** Can be climbed with Little Annapurna in one day
- Combined distance: ~22 miles
- Combined elevation gain: ~7,000 feet
- Best for acclimatized parties with early start

#### 5. Little Annapurna (8,440') — Bulger Peak
**Route Type:** Alpine hiking/scramble

**Difficulty:** Up to Class 2 hike; "easiest Bulger Peak"
- "No more than a steep Class II hike"
- Trail up west side; "steep but nothing intimidating" slopes

**Access**
- 40-minute hike from Inspiration Lake basecamp
- Excellent warm-up climb for early-season expeditions
- Often summited at sunset on first evening

#### 6. Enchantment Peak (8,520') — Bulger Peak
**Route Type:** Alpine scramble with technical summit block

**Difficulty:** Class 3/4 scramble on summit block
- Highest of the three Bulger peaks in area
- Unique finish with "small, yet exceptional, summit block"
- Described as "easily the most rewarding part of entire trip"

**Base Camp Strategy**
- Position basecamp "just above Inspiration Lake" for day ascents
- Centrally located for accessing all three Bulger peaks

### Climbing Basecamp Strategy

**Ideal Multi-Peak Approach**
- Establish basecamp just above Inspiration Lake or near Crystal/Perfection saddle
- Central location allows day ascents of McClellan Peak, Little Annapurna, and Enchantment Peak
- Core Zone permit required
- Trip duration: 3+ days minimum for acclimatization and objectives

**Early-Season Opportunity**
- Overnight permits only required May 15–Oct 31
- Access via Snow Lakes or Stuart Lake trails before May 15 without permits
- Spring expedition possible with mountaineering equipment (crampons, ice axes)
- Snowshoes may be necessary

### Rock Climbing Areas
- **Rock Type:** Granite throughout
- **Popular Multi-Pitch Areas:** Alpine rock on surrounding peaks accessible from Core Zone
- Snow Creek Wall accessible from lower basin
- Limited bolted sport climbing; traditional and alpine rock climbing emphasis

### Summary of Not-Yet-Documented Objectives
In the basin context, the following are significant climbing objectives not yet documented in ClimbMatch:
- **Aasgard Pass:** Dangerous alpine scramble/pass; critical for Core Zone access
- **Dragontail Peak:** Technical alpine scramble with Class 3/4 sections
- **Colchuck Peak:** Accessible scramble from base camp
- **McClellan Peak:** Class 3 Bulger summit
- **Little Annapurna:** Easiest Bulger Peak; Class 2 approach
- **Enchantment Peak:** Class 3/4 technical Bulger summit
- **Snow Creek Wall:** Rock climbing area accessible from lower basin

## Key Takeaways

1. **Permits are Essential:** Overnight permits (May 15–Oct 31) are highly competitive; apply in February for best chances. Day-use permits are free and self-issued.

2. **Aasgard Pass is Dangerous:** Not a maintained trail; requires scrambling experience, good route-finding, and can be life-threatening. Snow coverage varies; spring attempts require mountaineering equipment.

3. **Peak Season is July-August:** Best weather and accessibility; highest crowds and most expensive permits.

4. **Shoulder Seasons Offer Value:** June and September/early October provide fewer crowds, still-good conditions, and lower permit pressure (use daily lottery).

5. **Road Closure Matters:** FR 7601 closes November–May; only Snow Lakes Trailhead is reliably accessible earlier in season.

6. **Water is Abundant in Upper Basin:** Fill up before descending from Snow Lakes; lower trail sections can be dry.

7. **Mosquitoes Peak in July-August:** Bring head nets and repellent; less severe in shoulder seasons.

8. **Basecamp Above Inspiration Lake Optimal:** Central location for summiting multiple Bulger peaks and accessing Core Zone objectives.

9. **Nearby Services Excellent:** Leavenworth has full infrastructure for pre-trip and post-trip resupply and lodging.

10. **Climbing Objectives Significant:** Aasgard Pass, Dragontail, and three Bulger peaks offer valuable mountaineering and alpine scrambling experiences not yet documented in climbing databases.
