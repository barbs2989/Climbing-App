# Hazard Taxonomy for watch_out Documentation

Standard hazard categories and documentation patterns for consistent, comprehensive route hazard warnings.

## Primary Hazard Categories

### 1. Serac & Icefall Hazards

Pattern: "Serac collapse hazard on [location] ([timing] exposure, [seasonal risk])"

Examples:
- "Serac collapse hazard on Fuhrer Finger headwall (10am-2pm exposure, July-Aug highest risk)"
- "Active icefall zone on Park Glacier (check conditions before committing, no stable climb window)"
- "Potential serac collapse on East Buttress (retreat route hazard — check before leaving fixed anchors)"

Key elements:
- Specific location (headwall, glacier name, pitch number)
- Time-of-day exposure window (10am-2pm, afternoon, dawn)
- Seasonal risk periods (March-May, July-Aug, spring)
- Frequency assessment (active, potential, historical)

### 2. Avalanche Exposure

Pattern: "Avalanche exposure [terrain angle] terrain ([seasonal trigger], [aspect])"

Examples:
- "High avalanche exposure >35° terrain (spring corn cycle, March-May, south/southwest aspect)"
- "Active avalanche path on approach (check NWAC forecast, time crossing for safety)"
- "Avalanche danger increased post-precipitation (24-48 hour stability assessment recommended)"

Key elements:
- Terrain angle (>35°, steep gullies, slope angle)
- Seasonal triggers (spring corn, rapid warming, wind-loaded, post-precip)
- Aspect (north, south, southwest, wind-loaded aspects)
- Mitigation (check forecast, time crossing, party management)

### 3. Crevasse Fields & Bergschrund

Pattern: "Crevasse field [location] ([seasonal dates], [crossing technique])"

Examples:
- "Crevasse field mid-June through August — bergschrund crossing critical early season"
- "Multiple transverse crevasses on Lower Curtis Glacier — requires glacier rope and crevasse rescue kit"
- "Bergschrund crossing may require rappel setup early season — inspect ice conditions"

Key elements:
- Location (glacier name, specific zone)
- Seasonal dates (when crevasses are open/manageable)
- Crossing technique (rope required, microspikes, rappel anchors)
- Hazard severity (hidden, large, technical)

### 4. Ice Quality & Conditions

Pattern: "[Ice type] ice [location] ([seasonal variation], [tool requirement])"

Examples:
- "Thin or absent ice most winters — be ready to dry-tool or abandon route"
- "Blue ice sections P3-P4 — microspikes insufficient, ice tools required"
- "Snow ice/verglas common — crampon penetration and tool placement unreliable"
- "Condition-dependent climbing (varies dramatically year-to-year)"

Key elements:
- Ice type (blue ice, snow ice, verglas, thin, waterfall ice)
- Location (pitch number, section)
- Seasonal variation (condition changes)
- Tool requirement (microspikes only, ice tools, dry-tool option)

### 5. Rockfall & Rock Quality

Pattern: "[Rock quality] rock on [location] — [hazard consequence]"

Examples:
- "Loose, chossy rock on pitch 2 and through box gully — protection difficult, rockfall exposure"
- "Significant rockfall below the headwall — move quickly through exposure"
- "Poor rock quality at some belay/rappel anchors — inspect thoroughly, may need fresh anchors"
- "Loose blocks on the towers — loose rock and rockfall hazard throughout"

Key elements:
- Rock quality assessment (loose, chossy, unstable)
- Specific location (pitch number, section)
- Consequence (protection difficult, rockfall exposure)
- Mitigation (move quickly, helmets required, inspect anchors)

### 6. Route-Finding & Navigation

Pattern: "Route-finding [difficulty] ([condition], [consequence])"

Examples:
- "Route-finding critical in whiteout — carry map/GPS/compass"
- "Complex route-finding through icefall in low visibility — careful navigation required"
- "Multiple line options at similar grade (Thin Column vs. East Column) — choose based on party ability"
- "Wrong-summit errors possible — study map before committing"

Key elements:
- Difficulty level (critical, complex, multiple options)
- Conditions that impact routing (whiteout, low visibility, darkness)
- Consequences (navigation errors, wrong peak, major delays)
- Mitigation (navigation tools, pre-plan route, study maps)

### 7. Descent Hazards & Technical Difficulty

Pattern: "Descent [technique] ([complexity], [anchor quality])"

Examples:
- "Descent via rappels on snow/ice — anchor quality variable, inspect thoroughly, may need fresh anchors"
- "No established rappel stations on descent — force ad hoc anchors in poor rock, inspect thoroughly"
- "Descent complexity in poor visibility — be prepared for navigation errors and delayed exit"
- "Multiple rappels required — cumulative hazard of many anchors, check each one"

Key elements:
- Descent technique (rappels, scrambling, downclimbing)
- Technical complexity (multiple rappels, traverse, route-finding)
- Anchor quality assessment (variable, poor, inspect)
- Time/energy consequences (extended descent, darkness risk)

### 8. Altitude & Commitment Hazards

Pattern: "[Type] at [altitude] ft — [consequence]"

Examples:
- "High altitude (14,410 ft) — altitude sickness common on repeated attempts"
- "Long commitment — limited escape options once past pitch 3"
- "High commitment alpine route — turnaround time critical, plan descent carefully"
- "Remote location with limited bailout options — self-rescue knowledge required"

Key elements:
- Altitude (feet, meters, risk zone)
- Commitment level (high, long, limited escape)
- Time consequence (late start consequences, darkness risk)
- Required knowledge (self-rescue, alpine skills)

### 9. Weather & Environmental Hazards

Pattern: "[Weather type] common ([season], [consequence])"

Examples:
- "Summit wind 40+ knots typical — expect summit conditions worse than forecast"
- "Afternoon thunderstorms common — rapid turnaround required"
- "Cold northwest-facing aspect — expect winter temperatures at belays even on clear days"
- "Wind-loaded aspects prone to avalanche — check forecast for wind direction and speed"

Key elements:
- Weather type (wind, storms, cold, icing)
- Seasonality (summer afternoons, winter, spring)
- Magnitude (40+ knots, frequent, typical)
- Consequence (turnaround time, exposure, temperature management)

### 10. Technical Difficulty & Mixed Terrain

Pattern: "[Difficulty] climbing ([grade], [technique])"

Examples:
- "Technical mixed climbing IV+/M5 — requires ice/rock tool proficiency"
- "Mixed terrain transitions (snow/ice/rock) — crampon/axe removal/redonning required multiple times"
- "Sustained ice sections WI4-5 throughout — challenging tool placement, exposure"
- "Grade progression non-linear (easier 1st pitch, crux pitch 3, easier exit)"

Key elements:
- Technical grade (M, mixed, WI)
- Specific pitches/sections (crux location)
- Technique requirement (ice tools, mixed technique)
- Skill consequence (ability level matching)

### 11. Exposure & Consequences

Pattern: "[Exposure type] on [location] — [consequence if fall]"

Examples:
- "Exposure on summit tower — one-hand-off belay standard, take care with anchors"
- "Significant exposure throughout route — fall consequence is fatal"
- "Loose rope management on multi-pitch — system errors have high consequence"

Key elements:
- Exposure type (technical, distance, terrain)
- Location (pitch number, section)
- Consequence severity (injury, fatality risk)

## Documentation Guidelines

### Structure & Formatting

Each hazard entry should:
1. **Start with hazard type/category** (serac, avalanche, crevasse, etc.)
2. **Include specific location** (pitch number, glacier name, peak section)
3. **Add temporal/seasonal context** (when, time-of-day, months)
4. **Describe consequence or impact** (what happens if not managed)
5. **Suggest mitigation when applicable** (check forecast, move quickly, inspect anchors)

### Length Guidelines

- **Minimum**: 15 characters (too short = lacks detail)
- **Optimal**: 100-200 characters (readable, specific)
- **Maximum**: 300 characters (longer = consider splitting into multiple entries)

Example ranges:
- SHORT (18 chars): "Loose rock in box gully"
- GOOD (135 chars): "Loose, chossy rock on pitch 2 and through box gully — protection can be difficult, rockfall exposure below"
- LONG (220+ chars): "Active avalanche path crossing (Phantom Slide approach) — check NWAC Snoqualmie Pass forecast, time crossing carefully, avalanche patrol explosives nearby, stay clear of ski boundary"

### Tone & Language

- **Use active voice**: "Serac collapse hazard" vs. "Seracs may collapse"
- **Be specific**: "WI4-5 ice" vs. "challenging ice"
- **Include context**: "April-June" vs. "spring"
- **Use consistent terminology**:
  - Serac/icefall (prefer specific location)
  - Bergschrund (not bergschrund, don't use crevasse)
  - Verglas (not "thin ice coating")
  - Microspikes/crampons (not "traction devices")

### Seasonal Accuracy

Document hazards with 2026 context:
- Recent glacial changes (retreat, new crevasses)
- Climate impact on snow/ice conditions
- Updated NWAC research on avalanche patterns
- Recent incident documentation

### Source & Confidence

Each hazard should reference source:
- "Mountain Project" (most routes)
- "AAJ report" (incident/historical data)
- "Recent party report" (2024-2025)
- "Guide company beta" (RMI, IMG)
- "NWAC research" (avalanche-specific)

Confidence levels:
- **High**: Multiple recent sources, current conditions documented
- **Medium**: Historical data, some recent verification
- **Low**: Single source, needs verification

## Common Hazard Patterns by Route Type

### Ice Routes (WI2-WI5)

Typically include:
1. Thin/absent ice conditions
2. Loose rock quality
3. Route-finding complexity
4. Rappel anchor quality
5. Cold environmental conditions
6. Occasional avalanche/rockfall

### Alpine Routes (PD-D-AD)

Typically include:
1. Crevasse fields & bergschrund
2. Route-finding in low visibility
3. Altitude sickness risk
4. Exposed scrambling/3rd class
5. Weather exposure (wind, storms)
6. Mixed terrain transitions

### Technical Alpine (IV-V)

Typically include:
1. Serac/icefall hazards
2. Avalanche exposure (>35°)
3. Technical ice/mixed climbing
4. Long commitment
5. Complex descent
6. Altitude consequences

### Mixed Routes (M5-M7)

Typically include:
1. Protection quality (rock pitches)
2. Mixed terrain transitions
3. Loose rock exposure
4. Tool placement reliability
5. Exposure on crux pitches
6. Commitment level

## Quality Assurance

### Hazard Validation Checklist

- [ ] Specific location mentioned (not vague)
- [ ] Seasonal/temporal context included
- [ ] Consequence or impact described
- [ ] Mitigation suggested when applicable
- [ ] Consistent with other WA route hazards
- [ ] Matches known conditions (2026)
- [ ] Verifiable against Mountain Project/sources
- [ ] No redundant/duplicate hazards
- [ ] Appropriate tone (serious but not alarmist)

### Conflict Resolution

If hazard data conflicts between sources:
1. Prioritize recent (2024-2026) data
2. Use multiple source agreement
3. Flag conflicts with "[conflicting reports]"
4. Research further if high-consequence hazard
