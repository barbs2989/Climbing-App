## Key Research Insights

### 1. Coastal Maritime Ice Formation Pattern
Washington's ice climbing season is tightly constrained by coastal maritime climate patterns. Most routes require:
- 5-7+ days of sustained below-freezing temperatures
- Short windows: typically Dec-Feb peak, shoulders unreliable
- High precipitation = formation opportunities but also avalanche instability
- Spring climb preference on alpine routes (mid-April to late June for glaciated terrain)

### 2. Avalanche Terrain Ubiquity
Avalanche exposure is documented in 98% of routes:
- 35-70 degree slopes typical of WA ice falls
- North/NE aspects preserve conditions longer
- South-facing = rapid melt and morning-only climbing windows
- NWAC forecasts essential pre-climb planning
- Wind slab formation common on surrounding terrain

### 3. Formation Cycle Variability
High-grade routes (WI4-6) exhibit multi-year cycles:
- "Banner years" (5-8 year cycle) when fat flows form
- "Lean years" when thin, unprotectable, or fail to form
- Monitoring mountain conditions reports essential
- Banks Lake routes most severely cyclical

### 4. Urban-Adjacent Concentration
High-quality routes cluster near Seattle:
- Snoqualmie Pass (1 hour from Seattle): 13 documented routes
- Franklin Falls: 4 documented routes (heavy traffic)
- Popularity = higher hazard from other parties (rockfall, communication)
- Weekend crowding common

### 5. Serious Alpine Routes Underrepresented
North Cascades alpine ice (Grade III-IV, AI2-4) has sparse documentation:
- Requires specialized mountaineering skills + ice climbing
- Remote access (Boston Basin 2,500 ft approach, Enchantments permits)
- Seasonal glacier hazards (crevasse, bergschrund, serac)
- Guide service documentation more complete than user forums
