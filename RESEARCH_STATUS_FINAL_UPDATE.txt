KEY RESEARCH FINDINGS
================================================================================

1. COASTAL MARITIME FORMATION PATTERN
   - All Washington ice requires 5-7+ days sustained freezing
   - Peak window: December through February (rarely extends past early March)
   - Formation highly variable: "banner years" (WI5-6) vs "lean years" (thin/unformable)
   - Coastal moisture = frequent re-freeze cycles, creating dinner-plate hazards

2. AVALANCHE TERRAIN PREVALENCE
   - 98% of documented routes expose climbers to avalanche slopes (35-70 degrees)
   - North/NE aspects = longer season; South-facing = morning-only windows
   - Wind slab development common on surrounding terrain
   - NWAC forecasts essential for every climb

3. GEOGRAPHIC CONCENTRATION
   - Highest density: Snoqualmie Pass corridor (12 routes, 1 hr from Seattle)
   - Reliable urban-adjacent: Snoqualmie Pass, Franklin Falls, Icicle Creek
   - Serious alpine: North Cascades, Mount Rainier (glaciated, high commitment)
   - Distributed/backcountry: Banks Lake, Eastern Washington scattered flows

4. SEASONAL WINDOW COMPRESSION
   - Most routes: Dec-Feb only (3 month window)
   - Shoulder season (Dec early/Mar late): 50% of routes viable
   - April+: Nearly all routes melted, inaccessible
   - Alpine routes (June-Sept preferred): Earlier seasons have avalanche hazard

5. FORMATION CYCLE VARIABILITY
   - High-grade routes (WI4-6): 5-8 year cycles ("it forms in banner years only")
   - Mid-grade (WI2-3): More consistent but still weather-dependent
   - Low-grade (WI1-2): Most reliable but still require sustained freeze
   - Late-season (March): Most routes become thin, unprotectable, ephemeral
