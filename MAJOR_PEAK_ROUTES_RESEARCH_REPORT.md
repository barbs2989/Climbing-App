## Mount Rainier Routes

### 1. Willis Wall
**Grade:** Grade V, M5+ X (Extreme)  
**Discipline:** Alpine Mixed  
**Coordinates:** 46.8529, -121.76047 (14,410 ft)

#### Key Details
- **Status:** One of the most fearsome alpine faces in the lower 48 states
- **Location:** Between Liberty Ridge and Curtis Ridge on Rainier's north face
- **Approach:** Via Liberty Ridge base (White River entrance); requires navigation across Emmons, Winthrop, and Liberty Cap Glaciers
- **Approach Time:** 4 hours
- **Climb Time:** 10 hours
- **Total Time:** 20 hours with overnight camp

#### Route Characteristics
- 15 pitches of continuous mixed climbing (45-60 degrees)
- Sustained rock and ice sections on icy slopes
- Active serac fall hazard throughout climb
- Exposure rating: Extreme (X rating indicates serious objective danger)
- First ascent: 1954 by Charles Bell, Jens Klubberud, Loren Campbell

#### Hazards
1. **Active Seracs:** Falling ice strafe the wall continually—this is the primary hazard
2. **Mixed Climbing Exposure:** Steep ice-coated rock with high consequence
3. **Bergschrund Crossing:** Variable conditions season-dependent
4. **Crevasse Fields:** Multiple glaciers on approach require technical navigation
5. **Weather Exposure:** High altitude north face with limited escape options

#### Gear Requirements
- Ice screws (8-10), rock protection full alpine rack (pins, nuts, cams)
- Crampons, ice axes, helmet (required)
- 60m rope, belay device, harness
- Snow pickets, slings, webbing

#### Seasonal Considerations
- **Best Months:** June-July (short window)
- **Permit:** Mount Rainier climbing permit required (NPS)
- **Key Factor:** Serac activity varies; morning ascents preferred to minimize rockfall
- **Notes:** Route rarely climbed due to extreme hazards and technical difficulty

**Assessment:** Likely already in database as "Willis Wall" variant. Verify current entry and update with complete hazard/gear documentation if missing details.

### 2. Central Mowich Face
**Grade:** Grade IV, AI2-AI3, 50-60 degree snow  
**Discipline:** Alpine Ice/Snow  
**Coordinates:** 46.8529, -121.76047 (13,000 ft)

#### Key Details
- **Location:** Northwest face of Rainier above Mowich Lake
- **Approach:** Wonderland Trail to Spray Park (4 miles), climbers trail up Ptarmigan Ridge, descend to North Mowich Glacier at 8,300 ft
- **Approach Time:** 5 hours
- **Climb Time:** 6 hours  
- **Total Time:** 15 hours with overnight camp

#### Route Variations
**Variation 1 - Grade III (Standard Route)**
- Cuts left at 12,500 ft to hourglass (snow/ice/rock section)
- Snow ramp 40-50 degrees with rock band exposure
- Requires rock scrambling but less technical ice

**Variation 2 - Grade IV (Ice Route)**
- Cuts right at 12,500 ft to ice steps (AI2-3)
- 4-6 pitches steep snow at 50-60 degrees
- More sustained ice climbing

#### Hazards
1. **Steep Snow/Ice:** 40-60 degree slopes with exposure
2. **Hourglass Section:** Rock and ice mix with rockfall exposure
3. **Rock Band:** Hazard when snow coverage insufficient
4. **Hidden Crevasses:** Lower glacier approach
5. **Afternoon Rockfall:** North face sun exposure (climb morning)
6. **Seracs:** Upper section exposure (less active than Willis Wall)

#### Gear Requirements
- Ice screws (4-6), snow pickets (2-4)
- Minimal rock gear (slings, hexes)
- Crampons, ice axes, helmet (required)
- 60m rope, belay device, harness

#### Seasonal Considerations
- **Best Months:** June-July
- **Key Factor:** Bergschrund may not be passable early season; bypasses through rock bands exist
- **Optimal Timing:** Morning climbs recommended
- **Permit:** Mount Rainier climbing permit required

**First Ascent:** 1968 by Dee Molenaar, Gene Prater, Jim Wickwire, Dick Pargeter

**Assessment:** Likely in database as "Central Mowich Face" (listed on Mountain Project). Verify completeness of hazard and gear documentation.

### 3. North Mowich Headwall (Edmunds Headwall)
**Grade:** Grade IV, WI3, 40-55 degree slopes  
**Discipline:** Alpine Ice/Mixed  
**Coordinates:** 46.8529, -121.76047 (13,500 ft)

#### Key Details
- **Location:** North side of Rainier via Mowich Lake
- **Approach:** Wonderland Trail to Spray Park (4 miles), climbers trail up lower Ptarmigan Ridge, descend to North Mowich Glacier at 8,300 ft. High camp ~10,000 ft
- **Approach Time:** 6 hours
- **Climb Time:** 8 hours
- **Total Time:** 19 hours with overnight camp

#### Route Characteristics
- 8 pitches mixed ice/snow/rock
- Prominent 40-50 foot hourglass section (rock/ice mix)
- Steep snow slope ~45 degrees for several thousand feet
- Rock band section requiring 5th class climbing (or natural bypasses)
- Upper ice cap with final snow walking to Liberty Cap
- Alternative: traverse under rock band to Central Mowich Headwall

#### Crux
- Hourglass section: ~1 hour to ascend
- Rock band: navigation or climbing required
- Steep snow slopes: sustained 45-degree climbing

#### Hazards
1. **Steep Ice/Snow:** 40-55 degrees with limited escape options
2. **Hourglass Feature:** Narrow section with rock/ice exposure
3. **Rock Band:** Exposed climbing with fall potential
4. **Hidden Crevasses:** Glacier approach
5. **Objective Hazard:** Rockfall possible
6. **Committing:** Limited exit options mid-route

#### Gear Requirements
- Ice screws (6-8), snow pickets (4-6)
- Rock gear (pins, nuts, cams for rock band)
- Crampons, ice axes, helmet (required)
- 60m rope, belay device, harness

#### Seasonal Considerations
- **Best Months:** June-July
- **Key Factor:** Rock band easier to climb with less snow coverage
- **Permit:** Mount Rainier climbing permit required

**First Ascent:** 1968 by Mike Heath, Dan Davis, Mead Hargis, Bill Cockerham

**Assessment:** Listed as "North Mowich Headwall" (WI3) on Mountain Project. Verify if in database; if present, update with detailed approach/hazard documentation.

## Mount Adams Routes

### 1. Avalanche Glacier
**Grade:** Grade II, AI2, 30-40 degree snow  
**Discipline:** Alpine Snow/Ice  
**Coordinates:** 46.2007, -121.5022 (12,276 ft)

#### Key Details
- **Status:** Steeper alternative to popular South Spur route
- **Trailhead:** Cold Springs Trailhead, Gifford Pinchot National Forest
- **Approach:** Round-the-mountain trail to northwest side; direct glacier access from southwest
- **Approach Time:** 4 hours
- **Climb Time:** 5 hours
- **Total Time:** 12 hours with overnight camp

#### Route Characteristics
- Direct glacier ascent (more direct than South Spur)
- 30-40 degree snow/ice slopes
- Moderate crevasse navigation required
- Shorter vertical than South Spur but steeper slopes
- Upper icefall sections
- Descent: typically via South Spur or down-climb same route

#### Hazards
1. **Icefall:** Upper glacier sections exposed
2. **Crevasses:** Requires careful navigation and belaying
3. **Steeper Terrain:** More committing than South Spur route
4. **Loose Volcanic Rock:** Hazard on alternate descent options
5. **Hidden Crevasse Dangers:** Typical for glacier climbing

#### Gear Requirements
- Ice screws (4-6), snow pickets (4-6)
- Crampons, ice axe, helmet (recommended)
- 50-60m rope, belay device, harness
- First aid kit

#### Seasonal Considerations
- **Best Months:** June-July
- **Key Factor:** Snow coverage critical for stability and rock protection
- **Permit:** Mount Adams volcano permit required (USFS)
- **Notes:** More technical than South Spur; requires solid ice climbing skills

**Assessment:** Not commonly documented. May be missing from database—recommend adding with complete approach/hazard documentation.

### 2. Northwest Ridge (North Face of Northwest Ridge)
**Grade:** Grade II, AI2, 30-40 degree slopes  
**Discipline:** Alpine Snow/Ice  
**Coordinates:** 46.2007, -121.5022 (12,276 ft)

#### Key Details
- **Status:** Intermediate route, less crowded than South Spur
- **Trailhead:** Killen Creek Trailhead, Gifford Pinchot National Forest (5.7 miles via Forest Service Road 2329)
- **Approach:** Killen Creek Trail (#113) to PCT, then High Camp Trail (#10) to meadow camp (6,900 ft)
- **Approach Time:** 3 hours (shorter than South Spur)
- **Climb Time:** 5 hours
- **Total Time:** 11 hours with overnight camp

#### Route Characteristics
- Aesthetic alternative to South Spur
- Lower section: 30 degree snow/rock (less technical)
- Middle section: steepens to ~40 degrees at 10,000 ft
- Upper section: moderate snow climbing to summit
- Direct and relatively short route
- Good views and less crowding than South Spur

#### Crux
- Upper 40-degree sections
- Mostly simul-climbable on moderate slopes

#### Hazards
1. **Crevasses:** Glacier approach
2. **Steepening Sections:** Require focused climbing
3. **Weather Exposure:** North-facing aspect
4. **Volcanic Rock:** Loose rock in lower sections

#### Gear Requirements
- Ice screws (4-5), snow pickets (4-6)
- Crampons, ice axe, helmet (recommended)
- 50m rope, belay device, harness
- First aid kit

#### Seasonal Considerations
- **Best Months:** June-July-August
- **Key Factor:** North aspect maintains snow longer than South Spur
- **Permit:** Mount Adams volcano permit required (USFS)
- **Notes:** Can be climbed later in season than South Spur; cooler north-facing approach

**Assessment:** Listed on Mountain Project and The Mountaineers. Verify database coverage; if present, update with complete gear/approach documentation.

## Mount Stuart Routes

### 1. West Ridge
**Grade:** Grade II, 5.4 (crux 5.6)  
**Discipline:** Alpine Rock  
**Coordinates:** 47.4751, -120.9031 (9,415 ft)

#### Key Details
- **Status:** Probably second most popular technical route after North Ridge
- **Approach:** Teanaway River Road to trail end (23 miles from SR-970). Trail to Ingalls Pass (6,480 ft), continue to Ingalls Lake, ascend to West Ridge base
- **Approach Time:** 4 hours
- **Climb Time:** 5 hours
- **Total Time:** 12 hours (day climb possible)

#### Route Characteristics
- 8 pitches of alpine rock climbing
- Long and complicated route with many features
- Well-protected with varied climbing difficulty
- Summit pyramid contains crux (5.6 well-protected sections)
- Scenic ridge climbing with alpine exposure
- Can be climbed as day route by fast parties

#### Key Challenge
- Route-finding: many opportunities to wander off intended line into harder (Grade III/IV) terrain
- Requires solid alpine rock skills and navigation ability

#### Crux
- Summit pyramid: couple of 5.6 sections (well-protected)

#### Hazards
1. **Route-Finding:** Easy to get off route
2. **Exposed Ridge:** High altitude exposure
3. **Summit Crux:** Moderate fall potential on 5.6 sections
4. **Loose Rock:** Typical of alpine environment
5. **Weather Exposure:** Ridge position vulnerable

#### Gear Requirements
- Rock rack: hexes, cams (0.5-3 inch), stoppers
- 10-12 carabiners, slings and webbing
- 50-60m rope, belay device, harness
- Helmet (recommended)

#### Seasonal Considerations
- **Best Months:** July-August-September (snow-free)
- **Permit:** Day use acceptable (Alpine Lakes Wilderness permit)
- **Key Factor:** Alpine weather can change rapidly
- **Notes:** Afternoon weather typical; best climbed early in day

**Assessment:** Likely in database as West Ridge. Verify completeness of route beta and gear documentation.

### 2. Cascadian Couloir (Standard Route)
**Grade:** Class 3, Non-Technical  
**Discipline:** Alpine Scramble  
**Coordinates:** 47.4751, -120.9031 (9,415 ft)

#### Key Details
- **Status:** Standard and probably most popular route on Mount Stuart
- **Route Type:** Non-technical scramble
- **Approach:** Esmeralda Trail (0.5 miles), Ingalls Way Trail, Long's Pass Trail/Ingalls Pass junction, right to Long's Pass (6,300 ft)
- **Approach Time:** 3 hours
- **Climb Time:** 3 hours
- **Total Time:** 8 hours (day climb)

#### Route Characteristics
- Over 4,600 feet of Class 3 rock scrambling
- Southeast couloir ascent via moderate scramble terrain
- False summit plateau (9,200 ft)
- Final Class 2-3 scrambling to main summit
- Low exposure when on route
- No technical pitches required

#### Route Navigation
- Key challenge is staying on Class 3 terrain
- Easy to wander off-route into harder (4th-5th class) climbing
- Marker cairns help with route-finding

#### Hazards
1. **Route-Finding:** Easy to wander off Class 3 terrain
2. **Loose Rock:** Volcanic terrain typical
3. **Weather Exposure:** Open slopes vulnerable to afternoon thunderstorms
4. **Afternoon Thunderstorms:** Common July-August
5. **Moderate Rockfall Hazard:** Helmet recommended despite non-technical nature

#### Gear Requirements
- Helmet (recommended for rockfall)
- Light pack with minimal gear
- Optional: Crampons (early season), trekking poles (descent)
- No climbing rope or protection required

#### Seasonal Considerations
- **Best Months:** July-August-September
- **Early Season:** May have snow patches into July
- **Key Factor:** Afternoon weather thunderstorms common
- **Permit:** Day use acceptable (Alpine Lakes Wilderness permit)

**Assessment:** Definitely in database (most popular Mount Stuart route). Verify data completeness and accuracy.
